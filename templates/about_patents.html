The NBER patent database made the U.S. Patent record widely available, fueling a dramatic increase in research on technology and innovation (see Hall, Trajtenberg, and Jaffe, 2001 – the work has received over 1370 citations on Google Scholar as of late December, 2011) [1]. This effort drastically reduced the barriers to entry and made patent data available to a larger community of researchers that lack the hardware or programming skills to access the raw data. Lee Fleming and Ron Lai, funded by three SciSIP awards1, worked to enable the disambiguation of individual inventor names from these data, by both the National Bureau of Economic Research. [2]

The patent data documented here result from three ways of identifying the correlation (as distinct from causality) between NSF funding and patenting activity.

  1. The patent directly cites an NSF award in the USPTO application
  2. The patentee is a previous recipient of NSF funding
  3. The patent cites a patent received by an NSF funding recipient

Patents are only one measure of the results of scientific research. The portfolio explorer will expand to include additional measures – including publications and other measures of scholarly work – in future releases. The active engagement of the scholarly community in SciENCV will be an important source of information about the scientific, economic and social results of scientific funding.
